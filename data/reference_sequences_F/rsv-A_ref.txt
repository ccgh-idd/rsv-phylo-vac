METHODS: SELECTION OF RSV REFERENCE STRAINS
 
Reference Strain Selection and Justification
 
For this study, three well-characterized respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) reference strains were selected to represent key prototypical sequences for comparative genomic analysis: RSV A2 (GenBank accession M74568), RSV Long (AY911262), and RSV Line 19 (FJ614813). The selection of these strains was based on their established roles as reference standards in RSV research, their historical significance, and their distinct biological characteristics.
 
RSV A2 Strain Selection
 
The A2 strain (GenBank accession M74568) was selected as the primary reference strain due to its status as the most widely used laboratory reference strain for RSV subgroup A (Collins et al., 1984). Originally isolated in 1961, the A2 strain has been extensively characterized and serves as the prototype sequence for RSV-A in the majority of published research (Johnson et al., 1987; Anderson et al., 1988). The complete genome sequence of A2 was among the first RSV sequences to be fully characterized, making it the de facto standard for comparative studies (Collins et al., 1984; Sullender et al., 1998). The A2 strain is used as the reference sequence in major databases including NCBI RefSeq (NC_038235) and has been the basis for structural studies of the RSV fusion protein, including both prefusion and postfusion conformations (McLellan et al., 2011; McLellan et al., 2013). Furthermore, A2 serves as the reference strain for the development of therapeutic monoclonal antibodies, including palivizumab, the only FDA-approved RSV prophylactic treatment (Johnson et al., 1997; The IMpact-RSV Study Group, 1998).
 
RSV Long Strain Selection
 
The Long strain (GenBank accession AY911262) was included as it represents the oldest available complete RSV-A genome sequence, with the original isolate dating from 1956 (Stott et al., 1960; Chanock et al., 1961). This strain is particularly valuable as it represents a wild-type field isolate that predates extensive laboratory passage, making it more representative of circulating RSV strains compared to the laboratory-adapted A2 strain (Stokes et al., 2011; Moore et al., 2009). The Long strain is designated as ATCC VR-26 and has been used as a reference in numerous epidemiological and evolutionary studies (Peret et al., 2000; Zlateva et al., 2005). Recent comparative studies have specifically used the Long strain as an alternative reference to A2 to ensure that findings are representative of field isolates rather than laboratory-adapted variants (Hause et al., 2017; Ramaekers et al., 2020). The inclusion of Long strain allows for assessment of sequence variability relative to both laboratory-adapted (A2) and wild-type (Long) backgrounds, providing a more comprehensive evolutionary context.
 
RSV Line 19 Strain Selection
 
The Line 19 strain (GenBank accession FJ614813) was selected due to its unique pathogenic properties and its value as a model for severe RSV disease (Stokes et al., 2011; Moore et al., 2009). Originally isolated in 1967 and subsequently passaged in primary chick cells, Line 19 exhibits enhanced pathogenesis in mouse models compared to standard laboratory strains, including increased viral loads, enhanced mucus production, and airway dysfunction that more closely resembles human RSV disease (Moore et al., 2009). The complete genome sequence of Line 19 revealed only 16 nucleotide differences compared to the Long strain, with 10 amino acid changes distributed across several proteins, including 6 changes in the fusion (F) protein (Moore et al., 2009). These minimal but functionally significant changes make Line 19 particularly valuable for structure-function studies and vaccine development research. The strain has been used extensively in preclinical studies to evaluate RSV vaccines and therapeutics due to its enhanced disease phenotype (Stokes et al., 2011; Hotard et al., 2012).
 
Rationale for Multi-Strain Reference Approach
 
The inclusion of multiple reference strains follows established best practices in viral genomics research, as recommended by the WHO RSV Global Surveillance Project for comprehensive RSV characterization (Goya et al., 2020; Ramaekers et al., 2020). Using multiple reference strains accounts for the inherent variability within RSV-A and provides a more robust framework for evolutionary and comparative analyses (Zlateva et al., 2005; Dapat et al., 2010). This approach is particularly important for RSV research given the documented effects of laboratory passage on viral genome sequences, where extended passage can introduce mutations that may not reflect the properties of wild-type circulating strains (Luongo et al., 2009; Hotard et al., 2015).
 
The three selected strains represent different aspects of RSV biology: A2 as the standard laboratory reference, Long as the wild-type prototype, and Line 19 as an enhanced pathogenicity variant. This combination provides comprehensive coverage for comparative genomic analyses while maintaining compatibility with the extensive body of existing RSV literature that utilizes these well-characterized reference strains.
